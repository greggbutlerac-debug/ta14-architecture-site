Buyer-side standard

Can the vendor prove admissibility before consequence?

Before an AI system is purchased, integrated, delegated authority, or allowed to create reliance, the buyer should ask whether consequence-bearing action would be admissible under the full TA-14 chain.

Not a vendor demo

Admissible evidence

What does the system know, where did it come from, and how is that knowledge preserved?

Admissible reliance

When may the system output be relied upon, refused, escalated, ignored, or treated as non-authoritative?

Admissible authority

Who or what may act, authorize, override, revoke, bind, commit, prevent, or execute?

Consequence formation

Can the vendor detect when consequence begins forming before binding, commit, or execution?

Fail-closed execution

Can the system stop under contradiction, missing evidence, invalid authority, drift, uncertainty, or scope breach?

Outcome and memory

What becomes record, precedent, new reality, residue if scoped, or future system memory?

Procurement risk classes

Severity before scope.

TA-14 uses risk classes to route the buyer to the minimum defensible scope before requesting deeper vendor evidence.

Class A through D
Risk Class Use When Default TA-14 Route
Class A — Information Only The AI provides non-binding information and does not create authority, commitment, routing, eligibility, access, payment, safety, or operational reliance. $49 Reviewability Check or $250 Governance Desk Review.
Class B — Reliance-Capable The AI output may influence a human decision, internal route, recommendation, escalation, or institutional expectation. $750 Addendum or $1,500 Buyer-Side Vendor Screen.

Routing principle

The more consequence can attach before execution, the less acceptable it is to rely on vendor claims without evidence objects, gate testing, and a written non-claim boundary.

Seven binary procurement gates

Plain tests before serious procurement.

These gates convert TA-14 architecture into a buyer-side procurement screen.

Pass / conditional / limited / refuse
1

Evidence Gate

Can the vendor prove what the system knows, where it came from, and whether it remained continuous?

2

Reliance Gate

Can the vendor prove when output may be relied upon and when reliance must be refused?

3

Authority Gate

Can the vendor prove who or what may authorize consequence-bearing action?

4

Consequence Formation Gate

Can the vendor detect when consequence begins forming before execution?

5

Binding / Commit Gate

Can the vendor prevent invalid binding or commit before action attaches?

6

Execution / Prevention Gate

Can the vendor execute only when admissible and fail closed when not?

7

Outcome / Memory Gate

Can the vendor govern what outcome, non-occurrence, new reality, residue, or memory carries forward?

Decision rule

Any high-risk AI system that cannot answer these gates should not move forward without condition, scope reduction, additional evidence, or TA-14 review.

Vendor response packet

Ask for evidence objects, not vague governance assurances.

The buyer should request a response packet containing reviewable evidence objects mapped to the route under review.

Evidence before confidence
Vendor Must Provide Minimum Response
Evidence lineage record Identify source, capture method, continuity, alteration controls, custody, and versioning.
Reliance boundary statement State when outputs may be relied upon, refused, escalated, ignored, or treated as non-authoritative.
Authority map Identify human, machine, institutional, policy, technical, override, revocation, binding, and commit authority.
Formation indicators Show how routing, reliance, expectation, dependency, or pressure is detected before execution.
Fail-closed proof Provide behavior under missing evidence, invalid authority, contradiction, drift, uncertainty, or scope breach.
Non-occurrence format Show how blocked or refused action is preserved as a record.
Known limits State what cannot be proven, what is outside scope, and what assumptions the buyer must not make.

Boundary

TA-14 may request evidence objects. TA-14 should not design the vendor-specific evidence architecture, proof objects, implementation roadmap, or route map for free.

Hard stop procurement triggers

Capability without admissibility should not become procurement confidence.

A hard stop does not mean the vendor is useless. It means procurement confidence is not admissible under the submitted evidence.

Pause / request evidence / reduce scope

No lineage

Vendor cannot explain what the system knows, where the knowledge came from, or whether continuity was preserved.

No reliance boundary

Vendor cannot state when output must be refused, ignored, escalated, or treated as non-authoritative.

No authority map

Vendor cannot show who or what may authorize, override, revoke, delegate, bind, commit, or execute.

No fail-closed proof

Vendor cannot prove what happens under contradiction, missing evidence, invalid authority, drift, or uncertainty.

No prevention record

Vendor cannot preserve why the system did not act or what harm was prevented.

No memory governance

Vendor cannot govern what residues, precedents, new realities, or memories carry forward.

Non-occurrence as procurement evidence

Most AI vendors try to prove what the system did. TA-14 asks whether the vendor can prove why the system was right not to act.

Prevented harm, blocked invalid action, avoided payment, refused access, halted routing, or non-deployment can have institutional value only if the non-occurrence is reviewable.

Refusal reason

Missing evidence, invalid authority, contradiction, drift, uncertainty, or scope breach.

Blocked action

What would have been routed, authorized, committed, executed, or relied upon.

Consequence avoided

What harm, exposure, invalid state, payment, access, denial, route, or reliance did not attach.
