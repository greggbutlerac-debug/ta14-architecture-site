# Evidence Integrity Evidence must remain trustworthy before a system is allowed to rely, bind, commit, execute, or create consequence. TA-14 does not begin at the moment of action. It begins earlier, with the evidence a system wants to rely on before consequence-bearing execution is allowed to proceed. Evidence Integrity is the upstream proof discipline inside **TA-14 Admissible Execution Architecture**. It asks whether records, signals, documents, logs, observations, measurements, outputs, claims, and determinations remain strong enough to become admissible evidence — and whether admissible evidence remains strong enough to support reliance. A record may exist. A log may exist. A dashboard may display a signal. A sensor may report a measurement. A model may produce an output. A workflow may preserve an event. A human may make a determination. But none of those things automatically prove that the material is trustworthy enough, continuous enough, authoritative enough, scoped enough, or admissible enough to support consequence. TA-14 separates the existence of information from the integrity of evidence. **No admissible evidence. No admissible reliance. No admissible execution.** --- ## Core Doctrine ## A record is not automatically admissible evidence. TA-14 separates: **Record → Evidence → Admissibility → Reliance → Authority → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome** That distinction matters because many systems collapse the chain. They assume that if something was logged, it is evidence. They assume that if something is evidence, it can be trusted. They assume that if evidence can be trusted, it can be relied upon. They assume that if reliance exists, execution may proceed. They assume that if execution is logged, governance happened. TA-14 does not accept that shortcut. A record must qualify as evidence. Evidence must preserve continuity. Admissibility must be earned. Reliance must be governed. Authority must be valid. Binding must be scoped. Commit must be controlled. Execution must be justified before consequence attaches. Outcome must be accountable after consequence occurs. Evidence Integrity is the discipline that protects the beginning of that chain. --- ## The Core Question Evidence Integrity asks: **Can the evidence still be trusted enough to support the consequence being proposed?** That question sits inside the full TA-14 chain: **Reality → Record → Continuity → Admissibility → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome** Evidence Integrity primarily protects the movement from: **Reality → Record → Continuity → Admissibility** It asks whether the system has preserved the trustworthiness of the evidence before moving into reliance, authority, binding, commit, execution, and outcome accountability. The question is not only: “Do we have a record?” The question is: **Can this record still carry consequence?** --- ## Why Evidence Integrity Matters Execution failures often begin as evidence failures. A system can execute the intended instruction and still misexecute if the evidence behind that instruction was incomplete, stale, broken, out of scope, unauthoritative, contaminated, contradicted, or insufficient for reliance. That is one of TA-14’s central findings: **A system can technically execute correctly and still misexecute.** Misexecution does not only occur when software malfunctions. Misexecution can occur when the system acts on evidence that should not have been relied upon. Bad evidence creates bad reliance. Bad reliance creates bad authority. Bad authority creates bad binding. Bad binding creates bad commit. Bad commit creates bad execution. Bad execution creates bad outcome. Once consequence attaches, the problem is no longer only informational. It becomes operational, institutional, environmental, financial, legal, human, or atmospheric. Evidence Integrity exists to stop damaged proof before it becomes consequence. --- ## Information, Record, Evidence, and Admissible Evidence TA-14 distinguishes several stages that are often treated as the same thing. ### Information Information is material available to a system. It may be a signal, message, measurement, output, claim, observation, document, log, image, statement, or event. Information is not automatically evidence. ### Record A record is preserved information. It may show that something was captured, stored, logged, written, measured, observed, or generated. A record is not automatically admissible evidence. ### Evidence Evidence is a record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness to a claim, decision, determination, intervention, or execution route. Evidence is not automatically admissible. ### Admissible Evidence Admissible evidence is evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, authoritative enough, and reviewable enough to support a defined reliance condition. Admissible evidence is not automatic authority to execute. ### Governed Reliance Governed reliance is the controlled point where admissible evidence may support a decision, route, boundary, permission, refusal, escalation, binding, commit, execution, or outcome claim. Reliance must be authorized and bounded. ### Execution Authority Execution authority is not created merely because evidence exists. Execution authority requires the evidence chain, reliance condition, authority basis, scope, binding, commit, and execution boundary to remain admissible. This is why TA-14 does not allow systems to jump from record to execution. --- ## The Dangerous Shortcut The dangerous shortcut is: **A record exists → therefore execution is allowed.** TA-14 rejects that shortcut. A record can exist and still fail as evidence. Evidence can exist and still fail admissibility. Admissible evidence can exist and still be insufficient for a specific reliance. Reliance can exist and still lack authority. Authority can exist and still be out of scope. Binding can occur too early. Commit can occur after evidence has degraded. Execution can proceed after the chain has already failed. Outcome can be claimed without proof that the consequence was admissibly produced. Evidence Integrity prevents systems from treating documentation as permission. The existence of data is not the same as admissible authority to create consequence. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Evidence Governance Evidence Integrity is the public-facing proof condition. Evidence Governance Architecture is the deeper TA-14 architecture family that explains how evidence itself must be governed. The page is called **Evidence Integrity** because that is the condition a reader can immediately understand: Can the evidence still be trusted? But the deeper architecture question remains: **What must be true of evidence before admissibility can exist?** Evidence Governance Architecture addresses that upstream question. It does not rename TA-14. TA-14 remains **Admissible Execution Architecture**. Evidence Governance explains what must be true before admissible execution can exist. Evidence Integrity describes the condition that must be preserved so evidence can support admissibility, reliance, and execution. Together, they protect the proof layer before consequence-bearing action occurs. --- ## Evidence Integrity Dimensions Evidence Integrity is not a single checkpoint. It is a set of connected integrity conditions that must survive across the chain. --- ## Source Integrity Source Integrity asks where the record, signal, observation, measurement, log, output, claim, or determination originated. A system must be able to explain the origin of the evidence. Key questions include: - Who or what produced the evidence? - Was the source identifiable? - Was the source authorized? - Was the source competent for the condition being measured or described? - Was the source preserved in the record? - Is the source still valid at the time of reliance? - Has the source been altered, replaced, contradicted, or invalidated? Without source integrity, evidence may appear useful while lacking foundation. A system should not rely on evidence if it cannot explain where the evidence came from. --- ## Chronology Integrity Chronology Integrity asks whether the order of events was preserved. TA-14 is chronology-first because consequence-bearing execution depends on what happened before what. The system must know whether observation happened before determination, whether determination happened before intervention, whether intervention happened before outcome, and whether outcome was recorded after action. Chronology failure occurs when records exist but sequence is unclear. This creates false admissibility. A system may know that several things happened, but not know the order in which they happened. When chronology breaks, admissibility weakens. --- ## Record Integrity Record Integrity asks whether the preserved record is complete, attributable, time-situated, source-aware, and reviewable. A record must do more than exist. It must preserve enough context to support later review. Record Integrity asks: - What was recorded? - When was it recorded? - Who or what recorded it? - Was the method known? - Was the record altered? - Was the record preserved? - Is the record linked to the condition it claims to represent? - Can the record be reviewed later? A record without source, timing, sequence, scope, or method may be available information, but it may not have enough integrity to support consequence. --- ## Baseline Integrity Baseline Integrity asks whether the system knows the starting condition. Without baseline, change is difficult to prove. In environmental systems, baseline may describe indoor air conditions, equipment performance, temperature, humidity, pressure, particulate levels, refrigerant behavior, ventilation state, or other atmospheric or mechanical conditions before intervention. In AI or institutional systems, baseline may describe the prior state of a record, authority condition, user scope, risk posture, workflow state, or operational condition before action. Baseline Integrity asks: - What was the prior condition? - Was it recorded? - Was it trustworthy? - Was it current? - Was it preserved? - Can later change be compared against it? Without baseline integrity, systems may claim improvement, degradation, compliance, risk, correction, or success without proof of what came before. --- ## Threshold Integrity Threshold Integrity asks whether the system knows what level, condition, rule, or boundary matters. Evidence is not meaningful without interpretive thresholds. A temperature reading, particulate count, confidence score, risk level, authority state, or compliance signal does not speak for itself. The system must know what threshold turns information into a meaningful determination. Threshold Integrity asks: - What threshold applies? - Who set it? - Is it documented? - Is it appropriate for the context? - Is it current? - Is it tied to authority? - Is it tied to consequence? - Was the threshold crossed before action occurred? Without threshold integrity, systems may act on undefined or unstable standards. --- ## Determination Integrity Determination Integrity asks whether the system’s interpretation of evidence is justified. Evidence does not automatically determine what should happen. Someone or something interprets it. That interpretation may be made by a human, an AI model, an automation rule, a diagnostic procedure, a policy engine, or a review process. Determination Integrity asks: - What was determined? - What evidence supported the determination? - Was the determination declared before action? - Was the determination within authority? - Was uncertainty preserved? - Were alternative explanations considered? - Was contradiction handled? - Was the determination strong enough for the proposed consequence? A declared determination is especially important in environmental, diagnostic, institutional, and AI-mediated contexts. A system should not move from reading to intervention without a reviewable determination. --- ## Continuity Integrity Continuity Integrity asks whether the evidence remained connected from reality to record to review to reliance to execution to outcome. Continuity is not just storage. Continuity is the preservation of evidentiary meaning over time. Evidence can lose integrity when it moves between systems, people, formats, processes, interpretations, or decision stages. Continuity risks include: - missing timestamps - broken sequence - changed source - overwritten records - unexplained edits - partial logs - undocumented handoffs - stale conditions - conflicting evidence - separated baseline - missing threshold - authority drift - incomplete outcome records Continuity Integrity protects against the quiet collapse of the evidence chain. A record may still exist while the chain is no longer trustworthy. --- ## Sufficiency Integrity Sufficiency Integrity asks whether the evidence is strong enough for the decision, route, intervention, reliance, or execution being considered. Not every consequence requires the same evidentiary strength. A low-risk internal routing step may require limited proof. A high-impact environmental, operational, financial, legal, institutional, or AI-mediated consequence may require stronger proof. Sufficiency Integrity asks: - What consequence is being proposed? - How much evidence is required? - Is the evidence complete enough? - Is the evidence continuous enough? - Is the evidence authoritative enough? - Is the evidence current enough? - Is the evidence pressure-survivable enough? - Can it carry this action? A system enters sufficiency risk when weak evidence is asked to carry heavy consequence. TA-14 treats that as a serious misexecution surface. --- ## Authority Integrity Authority Integrity asks whether the evidence and its use are supported by valid authority. Evidence may be accurate but still not usable for a particular action. Authority Integrity asks: - Who is allowed to create the evidence? - Who is allowed to validate it? - Who is allowed to interpret it? - Who is allowed to rely on it? - Who is allowed to bind a decision to it? - Who is allowed to commit execution based on it? - Who is allowed to execute from it? - Has that authority changed? - Is the authority limited by scope? - Is the authority still valid at the time of action? Authority drift is a major risk. A system may rely on evidence that was valid under one authority condition but not under another. TA-14 treats authority as part of the admissibility chain. --- ## Scope Integrity Scope Integrity asks whether evidence is being used only for the purpose it can support. Evidence valid for one claim may not be valid for another. A sensor reading may support a monitoring alert but not a diagnostic conclusion. A model output may support review but not autonomous action. A public architecture statement may support a public-material review but not a claim about private production implementation. A maintenance record may support historical context but not current operating condition. Scope Integrity asks: - What does this evidence actually prove? - What does it not prove? - What action is being justified? - Is the proposed use within the evidence’s scope? - Is the evidence being stretched beyond its capacity? Scope failure occurs when systems use limited evidence to justify broader consequence. --- ## Survivability Integrity Survivability Integrity asks whether evidence can remain valid under pressure. Evidence must survive the conditions in which it will actually be used. Those conditions may include: - runtime urgency - automation speed - procedural momentum - downstream dependency - escalation delay - rollback cost - partial execution - conflicting inputs - authority drift - changed conditions - institutional pressure - environmental volatility - adversarial pressure - multi-system handoff Evidence that looks sufficient during calm review may fail under real operating pressure. TA-14 asks whether the evidence remains admissibly governable when consequence is approaching. If evidence cannot survive pressure, it cannot safely support execution. --- ## Lifecycle Integrity Lifecycle Integrity asks what state the evidence currently occupies. Evidence has a state. That state can change. Evidence may be: - active - partial - degraded - conflicted - superseded - stale - insufficient - not reviewable Execution should not rely on evidence without knowing its current state. ### Active Current enough, connected enough, and reviewable enough to participate in admissibility analysis. ### Partial Useful but incomplete. May support investigation, but not consequence-bearing execution by itself. ### Degraded Weakening due to age, missing continuity, uncertainty, pressure, source issues, ambiguity, or changed conditions. ### Conflicted Competing records or interpretations prevent direct reliance without reconciliation or escalation. ### Superseded Replaced by stronger, newer, corrected, or higher-authority evidence. ### Stale Once useful but no longer current enough to support reliance for the proposed route. ### Insufficient Not strong enough to support the claim, threshold, diagnosis, authority, or execution route. ### Not Reviewable Lacks enough origin, continuity, source, context, or preservation to participate in admissibility review. If the system does not know whether evidence is active, partial, degraded, conflicted, superseded, stale, insufficient, or not reviewable, then the route is not ready for consequence-bearing execution. --- ## Evidence-to-Admissibility Transition The key transition is not data to action. The key transition is evidence to admissibility. Many systems try to move from data directly to action. TA-14 inserts the missing governance sequence: **Record → Evidence → Admissible Evidence → Governed Reliance → Authority → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome** ### Record A preserved trace, signal, document, observation, log, measurement, statement, image, output, or event record. ### Evidence A record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness to a claim or decision. ### Admissible Evidence Evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, and authoritative enough to support a defined reliance condition. ### Governed Reliance The controlled point where admissible evidence may support binding, commit, execution, or outcome claims within defined limits. If that transition is skipped, the system may bind, commit, or execute on material that was never admissible. --- ## Evidence Outrun Evidence can outrun governance. Evidence Outrun occurs when evidence volume, speed, complexity, reliance pressure, downstream use, or procedural momentum exceeds the system’s ability to preserve continuity and determine admissibility. In an evidence outrun condition, the workflow keeps moving even though the evidentiary basis is incomplete, degraded, unresolved, or not yet admissible. Evidence outrun can occur when: - automation moves faster than review - alerts become actions before validation - model outputs become decisions before grounding - environmental readings trigger intervention before baseline proof - procedural momentum makes intervention difficult - downstream actors rely before evidence matures - partial records are treated as complete - stale evidence is treated as current - unresolved ambiguity is bypassed - authority drift is ignored - outcome proof is assumed rather than preserved Evidence outrun is dangerous because the system may appear efficient while its proof chain is collapsing. TA-14 treats evidence outrun as a serious pre-execution risk. The system is not ready to proceed merely because the workflow can proceed. --- ## Evidence Integrity Supports the TA-14 Chain Evidence Integrity supports every part of the admissible-execution chain. ### Reality The condition, event, state, action, or environment the evidence claims to represent. Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence remains connected to actual reality or only to a representation, summary, dashboard, interpretation, assumption, or derivative output. ### Record The preserved trace or source object that captures some aspect of reality. Evidence Integrity asks whether the record is attributable, time-situated, source-aware, and reviewable. ### Continuity The proof that the record remained connected, ordered, preserved, and reviewable. Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence survived time, handoff, pressure, contradiction, reinterpretation, and downstream use. ### Admissibility The determination that evidence is sufficient, scoped, authoritative, and reliable enough for reliance. Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence has actually crossed that threshold. ### Binding The point where reliance begins to attach to obligation, permission, decision, routing, or consequence. Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence can support the binding condition. ### Commit The controlled boundary where a route becomes difficult to reverse and must be receipted. Evidence Integrity asks whether evidence remains admissible now, not merely earlier. ### Execution The action that may proceed only if the evidence chain supports admissible execution. Evidence Integrity asks whether the system is acting from admissible proof or merely operational momentum. ### Outcome The preserved proof of what execution created, changed, prevented, escalated, or refused. Evidence Integrity asks whether the outcome can be accounted for after consequence occurs. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Reliance Evidence Integrity leads directly into Reliance Governance. Evidence Integrity asks: **Is the evidence still trustworthy?** Reliance Governance asks: **May the system now depend on it?** This distinction matters. Evidence may exist but not be reliable. Evidence may be reliable for one purpose but not another. Evidence may support a low-risk action but not a high-consequence one. Evidence may have been sufficient earlier but degraded before reliance occurred. TA-14 separates these questions because systems often fail by moving too quickly from “we have information” to “we can act.” Evidence Integrity prevents damaged evidence from becoming false confidence. Reliance Governance prevents premature use. Together, they protect the chain before consequence attaches. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Binding Binding is the point where evidence, authority, scope, and decision basis begin to attach to a consequence-bearing path. Evidence Integrity matters because binding should not occur unless the evidence can support it. Before binding, TA-14 asks: - Is the evidence trustworthy? - Is the source known? - Is chronology preserved? - Is the record intact? - Is the baseline known? - Is the threshold defined? - Is the determination reviewable? - Is continuity intact? - Is authority valid? - Is scope constrained? - Is the evidence healthy? - Is the evidence sufficient for the proposed consequence? Binding without Evidence Integrity is premature binding. Premature binding creates downstream misexecution risk. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Commit Commit is the point where the system moves from candidate consequence toward actual execution. Evidence Integrity matters because evidence may have changed before commit. Evidence may have been sufficient earlier but degraded later. Conditions may have shifted. Authority may have changed. A baseline may no longer hold. A threshold may have been crossed. Contradictory evidence may have appeared. Human escalation may still be unresolved. The workflow may be moving forward because of procedural momentum rather than admissible proof. Before commit, TA-14 asks whether evidence remains admissible now. Not earlier. Not in theory. Now. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Execution Execution is where consequence begins to attach. At execution, evidence weakness becomes consequence risk. A system may execute exactly as designed and still misexecute if the evidence basis was not admissible. Execution is not admissible merely because it followed a workflow. Execution is admissible only if the chain that allowed it to proceed was admissible. Evidence Integrity supplies the upstream trust condition for that determination. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Outcome Accountability Outcome is not just what happened after execution. Outcome is the final test of whether the evidence chain remained accountable. A system should be able to show: - what reality condition existed - what record was formed - how chronology was preserved - what baseline existed - what threshold applied - what determination was made - what evidence supported reliance - what authority allowed action - what scope limited action - what binding basis attached - what commit decision occurred - what execution followed - what outcome resulted - whether the outcome matched the admissible basis If the system cannot account for the outcome, it cannot prove that execution remained admissible. Evidence Integrity therefore reaches beyond record creation. It supports full outcome accountability. --- ## Evidence Integrity in AI Systems AI systems often rely on evidence chains that are difficult to see. A model may generate an answer, classification, recommendation, risk score, summary, or decision-support output. But TA-14 asks what evidence supported that output and whether the evidence retained integrity. For AI systems, Evidence Integrity asks: - What sources grounded the output? - Were those sources authoritative? - Were they current? - Were they within scope? - Was the relevant reality condition captured? - Was the evidence continuous from source to output? - Were contradictions detected? - Was uncertainty preserved? - Was the output treated as information or as evidence? - Was a person or system allowed to rely on it? - Did reliance occur before admissibility was established? - Was action blocked when evidence was insufficient? Many AI governance approaches focus on alignment, monitoring, explainability, fairness, safety, or compliance. Those are important. TA-14 adds a prior execution question: **Was the evidence chain admissible enough to allow the consequence-bearing action at all?** --- ## Evidence Integrity in Environmental Systems Environmental Integrity Governance is one of the clearest applied domains for Evidence Integrity. Air quality, HVAC performance, refrigeration behavior, ventilation, indoor environmental conditions, and atmospheric records all depend on trustworthy evidence. But ordinary monitoring is not enough. A dashboard reading is not automatically admissible environmental evidence. A sensor value is not automatically proof. A comfort complaint is not automatically a diagnostic determination. An intervention is not automatically justified because a system detected a condition. Environmental Integrity Governance requires a preserved environmental evidence chain. That chain may include: - observed environmental condition - sequence record - baseline - threshold - declared diagnostic determination - intervention authority - action record - post-intervention outcome - preserved atmospheric continuity This is where Evidence Integrity ties directly into the sequence, baseline, threshold, diagnostic determination, and post-intervention record structure. The issue is not simply whether a sensor detected something. The issue is whether the environmental evidence remained intact enough to justify reliance and intervention. --- ## Atmospheric Integrity Records Atmospheric Integrity Records apply Evidence Integrity to indoor air and environmental conditions. They preserve atmospheric evidence as more than monitoring data. An Atmospheric Integrity Record should help answer: - What condition existed? - When did it exist? - What source observed it? - What baseline applied? - What threshold mattered? - What determination was made? - What intervention occurred? - What changed afterward? - Was the outcome preserved? - Can the chain be reviewed later? This matters because environmental reliance often happens over time. People may rely on indoor air claims, safety claims, performance claims, remediation claims, ventilation claims, comfort claims, or compliance claims. Without Evidence Integrity, atmospheric claims may become disconnected from proof. Atmospheric Integrity Records preserve the evidence chain so environmental consequence does not rest on dashboard memory alone. --- ## The Observer, Actor, and Validator Problem Environmental systems face a special evidence-integrity risk. The same system may observe the condition, interpret the condition, intervene in the condition, and validate the outcome. That can be useful operationally. But it can also create evidentiary risk. If sensing, interpretation, intervention, and validation collapse into one uncontrolled loop, the evidence chain may become contaminated. The system may become observer, actor, and validator of its own claimed success. Evidence Integrity asks whether those roles remain reviewable. The goal is not to reject monitoring or automation. The goal is to preserve the distinction between operational action and admissible environmental proof. Monitoring can support evidence. Optimization can support performance. But admissible environmental reliance requires preserved integrity across observation, determination, intervention, and outcome. --- ## Evidence Integrity in Partner Reviews TA-14 partner reviews also depend on Evidence Integrity. Partner architectures may specialize in runtime governance, evidence maturity, pressure routing, trust layers, identity, enforcement, deterministic execution, controlled execution, environmental review, or other domains. TA-14 does not erase those architectures. It reviews how their evidence, authority, scope, binding, commit, execution, and outcome claims map into the full admissible-execution chain. Evidence Integrity is often the first review surface. Before TA-14 can evaluate whether a partner architecture supports admissible execution, it must ask: - What evidence does the partner system produce? - What evidence does it rely on? - What evidence does it preserve? - What evidence does it treat as authoritative? - What evidence does it require before action? - What happens when evidence is incomplete? - What happens when evidence is contradicted? - What happens when evidence degrades under pressure? - What happens when evidence is outrun by workflow momentum? - What evidence remains after outcome? A serious partner system may have strong governance in one part of the chain. TA-14’s role is to evaluate whether the full consequence-bearing chain remains admissible. Evidence Integrity is the upstream trust test. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Misexecution Misexecution occurs when a system allows consequence-bearing action that should not have been allowed under the admissible chain. A system can misexecute even when: - the software works - the workflow completes - the model returns an answer - the automation follows its rules - the human reviewer approves - the log records the event - the policy was technically followed TA-14 asks whether the execution should have been allowed. Evidence Integrity identifies misexecution risk before action occurs. Common evidence-integrity failure surfaces include: - incomplete evidence treated as sufficient - stale evidence treated as current - conflicting evidence ignored - authority-uncertain evidence used for binding - scope-limited evidence used beyond scope - degraded evidence used for commit - raw information treated as admissible proof - monitoring data treated as outcome evidence - model output treated as source evidence - procedural momentum outrunning evidence review - post-hoc documentation used to justify pre-action execution When Evidence Integrity fails, execution may become inadmissible even if the system performs as designed. --- ## Evidence Integrity Under Runtime Pressure Evidence Integrity becomes most important under pressure. In calm conditions, evidence gaps may be visible. Under runtime pressure, systems often move forward anyway. Pressure can come from: - urgency - automation speed - downstream reliance - operational dependency - business deadlines - customer impact - environmental risk - safety concerns - incomplete human review - escalation delay - rollback cost - partial execution - procedural momentum TA-14 asks whether admissible intervention remains real under those conditions. Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence itself remains trustworthy. If the evidence cannot be reviewed, challenged, refreshed, escalated, or used to block action before execution, then the system may be operating on theoretical admissibility rather than operational admissibility. That is not enough. A system must preserve Evidence Integrity when consequence pressure is highest. --- ## Evidence Integrity Maturity Path Evidence Integrity can be understood as a maturity path. A system may move through stages such as: 1. Raw information exists. 2. Information is recorded. 3. Record origin is known. 4. Chronology is preserved. 5. Baseline is established. 6. Thresholds are defined. 7. Determination is declared. 8. Continuity is protected. 9. Authority is validated. 10. Scope is constrained. 11. Contradictions are checked. 12. Evidence health is assessed. 13. Evidence sufficiency is matched to consequence. 14. Evidence survives pressure. 15. Evidence supports admissibility. 16. Admissibility supports reliance. 17. Reliance supports authority. 18. Authority supports binding. 19. Binding supports commit. 20. Commit supports execution. 21. Execution produces accountable outcome. 22. Outcome feeds back into the evidence chain. This maturity path prevents systems from jumping from data to action. TA-14 requires the evidence chain to mature before consequence attaches. --- ## Evidence Integrity Failure Modes Evidence Integrity identifies failure modes that ordinary governance may miss. ### Source Failure The evidence origin is unknown, weak, unauthorized, or unsuitable for the claim. ### Chronology Failure The system cannot prove the order of observation, determination, intervention, execution, and outcome. ### Record Failure The record exists but lacks enough source, timing, method, preservation, or context to support review. ### Baseline Failure The system cannot prove the starting condition. ### Threshold Failure The system cannot identify the standard, boundary, or condition that made the evidence meaningful. ### Determination Failure The system cannot show a clear, reviewable interpretation of the evidence before action. ### Continuity Failure The evidence chain breaks between observation, record, review, reliance, and execution. ### Sufficiency Failure The evidence is too weak to support the claim, threshold, diagnosis, authority, or execution route. ### Authority Failure The evidence lacks valid authority or relies on authority that has drifted. ### Scope Failure Evidence valid for one purpose is used for another. ### Survivability Failure The evidence cannot withstand runtime pressure, handoff, escalation, or partial execution. ### Outrun Failure The workflow advances faster than evidence can mature or be reviewed. ### Outcome Failure The system cannot account for the consequence after execution. These failures are not merely documentation problems. They are admissible-execution problems. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Public Materials TA-14 public reviews often begin from public materials. Public materials may include websites, documentation, publications, GitHub repositories, white papers, diagrams, product pages, standards claims, public posts, or published architecture descriptions. Evidence Integrity distinguishes between public signals and review evidence. Public materials can support a public-material architecture review. But TA-14 does not infer private capability from public claims. A public statement may be relevant evidence of positioning. It may not be sufficient evidence of implementation. A diagram may show architecture intent. It may not prove production behavior. A repository may show design artifacts. It may not prove route-complete runtime governance. A publication may support chronology. It may not prove current operational deployment. This distinction protects both the reviewer and the reviewed system. TA-14 can acknowledge strengths while identifying evidence gaps. That is the Evidence Integrity posture. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Production Readiness Production readiness requires more than promising architecture. It requires route-complete evidence. For consequence-bearing systems, TA-14 looks for evidence that routes are: - registered - governed - tested - fail-closed - source-grounded - authority-validated - scope-constrained - bind/commit-receipted - execution-controlled - replayable - outcome-accounted Evidence Integrity asks whether the evidence supporting those claims actually exists and remains trustworthy. A system may describe production readiness. TA-14 asks whether the evidence proves it. The production-readiness question is: **Can anything consequential happen outside the admissibility chain?** Evidence Integrity supplies the proof needed to answer that question. --- ## Evidence Integrity and Refusal Evidence Integrity is not only about allowing action. It is also about refusing action. A serious system must know when evidence is insufficient. TA-14 expects systems to refuse, block, escalate, or pause when Evidence Integrity is not strong enough. Evidence-based refusal may be required when: - evidence is missing - evidence is stale - evidence conflicts - authority is unclear - scope is exceeded - continuity is broken - baseline is absent - threshold is undefined - determination is unsupported - reliance would exceed evidence sufficiency - runtime pressure has degraded reviewability - outcome accountability cannot be preserved Refusal is not failure. In TA-14, refusal may be the correct admissible action. No admissible evidence. No admissible execution. --- ## Evidence Integrity Review Questions A TA-14 Evidence Integrity review may ask: - What evidence does the system claim to possess? - What reality condition does the evidence represent? - How was the evidence formed? - What is the evidence source? - Is the source authoritative? - Is the evidence time-situated? - Is chronology preserved? - Is the record intact? - Is baseline established? - Are thresholds defined? - Was a determination declared? - Is continuity protected? - Is scope constrained? - Is authority validated? - Is evidence health assessed? - Is sufficiency matched to consequence? - Can the evidence survive runtime pressure? - Can the system detect evidence outrun? - Does the system refuse action when evidence is insufficient? - Does the evidence support admissibility? - Does admissibility support reliance? - Does reliance support authority? - Does authority support binding? - Does binding support commit? - Does commit support execution? - Can the outcome be accounted for? - Can the full chain be reviewed after consequence? These questions help determine whether a system’s evidence layer is ready to support admissible execution. --- ## Evidence Integrity Decision Classes Evidence Integrity review can support practical TA-14-style findings. ### Strong Evidence Integrity The system demonstrates source-grounded, chronology-preserved, baseline-aware, threshold-governed, authority-valid, scope-constrained, pressure-survivable evidence. ### Partial Evidence Integrity The system preserves some evidence surfaces but lacks complete proof across continuity, authority, sufficiency, reliance, execution, or outcome. ### Emerging Evidence Integrity The system shows serious design intent but lacks route-complete evidence objects or implementation proof. ### Evidence Integrity Gap The system relies on information, logs, dashboards, outputs, or claims without proving that those materials retain evidentiary integrity. ### Evidence Outrun Risk The system appears capable of advancing toward consequence faster than evidence can mature, be reviewed, or support admissible reliance. ### Not Reviewable The available materials are insufficient to determine whether Evidence Integrity exists. These classes are not accusations. They are review findings. They help determine what evidence is present, what remains incomplete, and what would be required before consequence-bearing execution could be treated as admissible. --- ## Public Record Anchors Evidence Integrity is supported by the broader TA-14 public record. The public record includes architecture pages, indexed publication records, DOI-backed materials, GitHub repositories, public preservation pages, Environmental Integrity Governance materials, Atmospheric Integrity Records, and related TA-14 resource anchors.
