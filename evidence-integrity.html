Evidence Integrity

Evidence must be governed before execution.

TA-14 does not begin at the moment of action. It begins earlier, with the evidence a system wants to rely on before it binds, commits, executes, or creates consequence.

Evidence Integrity is the upstream proof discipline inside TA-14 Admissible Execution Architecture. It asks whether records, signals, documents, logs, observations, measurements, outputs, claims, and determinations are strong enough to become admissible evidence — and whether admissible evidence is strong enough to support reliance.

No admissible reliance.

Core chain

Record is not permission.

A system may possess a record and still lack evidence. It may possess evidence and still lack admissibility. It may possess admissible evidence and still lack governed reliance or authority to execute.

Record exists Evidence must qualify Admissibility must be earned Reliance must be governed Execution is not automatic

Evidence Integrity protects the movement from reality into admissible proof before consequence-bearing action occurs.

Core Doctrine

A record is not automatically admissible evidence.

TA-14 separates record, evidence, admissible evidence, reliance, authority, binding, commit, execution, and outcome.

Record → Evidence → Admissibility → Reliance → Authority → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome
What many systems assume

The shortcut

  • If it is logged, it is evidence.
  • If it is evidence, it can be trusted.
  • If it can be trusted, it can be relied upon.
  • If it can be relied upon, execution may proceed.
  • If execution is logged, governance happened.
What TA-14 requires

The admissible chain

  • A record must qualify as evidence.
  • Evidence must prove continuity and sufficiency.
  • Admissible evidence must be scoped to the decision.
  • Reliance must be authorized and bounded.
  • Execution must be controlled before consequence attaches.

The dangerous shortcut

When systems skip from “a record exists” to “execution is allowed,” they collapse the evidence chain. TA-14 exists to prevent that shortcut. The existence of data is not the same as admissible authority to create consequence.

Why It Matters

Execution failures often begin as evidence failures.

A system can execute the intended instruction and still misexecute if the evidence behind that instruction was incomplete, stale, broken, out of scope, unauthoritative, contaminated, contradicted, or insufficient for reliance.

01

Bad evidence creates bad reliance

If evidence is weak, unreliable, stale, incomplete, or contradicted, reliance built on that evidence is unstable.

02

Bad reliance creates bad execution

When a system relies on evidence that has not become admissible, it may bind, commit, or execute without a legitimate basis.

03

Bad execution creates bad outcome

Once consequence attaches, the problem is no longer only informational. It becomes operational, institutional, environmental, financial, legal, atmospheric, or human.

Evidence Integrity stops damaged proof before it becomes consequence.
The Distinction

Information, record, evidence, and admissible evidence are not the same thing.

Information A signal, statement, measurement, observation, output, message, image, claim, or event available to a system.
Record Preserved information. It may show that something was stored, logged, measured, written, captured, or generated.
Evidence A record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness to a claim or decision.
Admissible Evidence Evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, and authoritative enough to support a defined reliance condition.
Governed Reliance The controlled point where admissible evidence may support authority, binding, commit, execution, or outcome claims.

TA-14 does not allow systems to jump from record to execution. The evidence-to-admissibility transition must be preserved, governed, and reviewable.

Evidence Governance Architecture

Evidence itself requires governance.

Evidence Integrity is the public-facing proof condition. Evidence Governance Architecture is the deeper TA-14 architecture family that asks what must be true of evidence before admissibility can exist.

Source Integrity

Where did the record, signal, observation, measurement, log, output, claim, or determination originate, and is that origin trustworthy enough for review?

Chronology

Was the sequence preserved from reality to record to evidence to determination, or did the system lose the order needed to support consequence?

Continuity

Can the evidence prove it remained connected to the reality, source event, system state, or condition it claims to represent?

Sufficiency

Is the evidence strong enough for the actual decision, route, intervention, reliance, or execution being considered?

Authority

Who or what is authorized to generate, validate, interpret, rely on, bind, commit, or execute from this evidence?

Scope

What is the evidence allowed to prove, and what is outside its evidentiary reach?

Survivability

Can the evidence remain valid under runtime pressure, ambiguity, missing data, downstream reliance, adversarial pressure, or procedural momentum?

Lifecycle

Is the evidence active, partial, stale, superseded, degraded, conflicted, insufficient, or no longer reviewable?

Evidence Governance is upstream of execution

TA-14 remains Admissible Execution Architecture. Evidence Governance does not rename the parent. It explains what must be true before admissible execution can exist.

Evidence-to-Admissibility Transition

The key transition is not data to action. It is evidence to admissibility.

Many systems try to move from data directly to action. TA-14 inserts the missing governance sequence: record, evidence, admissible evidence, governed reliance, authority, binding, commit, execution, and outcome.

1

Record

A preserved trace, signal, document, observation, log, measurement, statement, image, output, or event record.

2

Evidence

A record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness to a claim or decision.

3

Admissible Evidence

Evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, and authoritative enough to support a defined reliance condition.

4

Governed Reliance

The controlled point where admissible evidence may support binding, commit, execution, refusal, escalation, or outcome claims within defined limits.

Evidence can outrun governance.

Evidence Outrun occurs when evidence volume, speed, complexity, reliance pressure, downstream use, or procedural momentum exceeds the system’s ability to preserve continuity and determine admissibility. TA-14 treats that as a serious pre-execution risk.

Evidence States

Evidence has a state. That state can change.

TA-14 treats evidence as something that can be active, partial, degraded, conflicted, superseded, stale, insufficient, or not reviewable. Execution should not rely on evidence without knowing its current state.

Active

Current enough, connected enough, and reviewable enough to participate in admissibility analysis.

Partial

Useful but incomplete. May support investigation, but not consequence-bearing execution by itself.

Degraded

Weakening due to age, missing continuity, uncertainty, pressure, source issues, ambiguity, or changed conditions.

Conflicted

Competing records or interpretations prevent direct reliance without reconciliation or escalation.

Superseded

Replaced by stronger, newer, corrected, or higher-authority evidence.

Stale

Once useful but no longer current enough to support reliance for the proposed route.

Insufficient

Not strong enough to support the claim, threshold, diagnosis, authority, or execution route.

Not Reviewable

Lacks enough origin, continuity, source, context, or preservation to participate in admissibility review.

If the system does not know the state of the evidence, the route is not ready for consequence-bearing execution.
TA-14 Chain Mapping

Evidence Integrity supports every part of the admissible-execution chain.

Reality

The condition, event, state, action, or environment the evidence claims to represent.

Record

The preserved trace or source object that captures some aspect of reality.

Continuity

The proof that the record remained connected, ordered, preserved, and reviewable.

Admissibility

The determination that evidence is sufficient, scoped, authoritative, and reliable enough for reliance.

Binding

The point where reliance begins to attach to obligation, permission, decision, routing, or consequence.

Commit

The controlled boundary where a route becomes difficult to reverse and must be receipted.

Execution

The action that may proceed only if the evidence chain supports admissible execution.

Outcome

The preserved proof of what execution created, changed, prevented, escalated, or refused.

Applied Domains

Evidence Integrity connects AI governance, environmental proof, and partner review.

AI Systems

A model output is not automatically admissible evidence. TA-14 asks what sources grounded the output, whether they were current and authoritative, whether uncertainty was preserved, and whether reliance occurred before admissibility was established.

Environmental Integrity

A dashboard reading is not automatically atmospheric proof. Environmental Integrity requires sequence, baseline, threshold, declared determination, intervention authority, and post-intervention record.

Partner Reviews

Partner architectures may specialize in one layer of governance. TA-14 reviews whether the full chain from evidence through outcome remains admissible before consequence is allowed.

The observer, actor, and validator problem

Environmental and automated systems can create evidentiary risk when the same operational loop observes the condition, interprets it, intervenes, and validates its own outcome. Evidence Integrity preserves the distinction between monitoring, optimization, operational action, and admissible proof.

Boundaries

Evidence Integrity is not a claim that every record is ready for execution.

Not all records are evidence

A record can exist and still fail as evidence if source, context, continuity, or meaning cannot be established.

Not all evidence is admissible

Evidence can be relevant but still insufficient, stale, conflicted, unauthorized, degraded, contradicted, or out of scope.

Not all admissible evidence authorizes execution

Even strong evidence must pass through reliance, authority, binding, commit, and execution control.

Not a legal evidence opinion

TA-14 evidence review is architecture and admissibility-chain review, not legal advice or a legal evidentiary ruling.

Not a cybersecurity audit

Evidence Integrity can surface integrity issues, but it does not replace cybersecurity, forensic, or compliance audits.

Not free implementation scope

Custom schemas, route objects, implementation guidance, maturity roadmaps, production mapping, proof-object design, and third-party implementation work require written scope.

Correct positioning
