Evidence Integrity

Evidence must be governed before execution.

TA-14 does not begin at the moment of action. It begins earlier, with the evidence that a system wants to rely on before it binds, commits, executes, or creates consequence.

Evidence Integrity is the upstream discipline that asks whether records, signals, documents, logs, observations, measurements, outputs, claims, and determinations are strong enough to become admissible evidence — and whether admissible evidence is strong enough to support reliance.

The distinction

Evidence does not become admissible just because it exists.

Record ≠ Evidence ≠ Admissible Evidence ≠ Reliance ≠ Execution Authority

What many systems assume

  • If it is logged, it is evidence.
  • If it is evidence, it can be trusted.
  • If it can be trusted, it can be relied upon.
  • If it can be relied upon, execution may proceed.
  • If execution is logged, governance happened.

What TA-14 requires

  • A record must qualify as evidence.
  • Evidence must prove continuity and sufficiency.
  • Admissible evidence must be scoped to the decision.
  • Reliance must be authorized and bounded.
  • Execution must be controlled before consequence attaches.

The dangerous shortcut

When systems skip from “a record exists” to “execution is allowed,” they collapse the evidence chain. TA-14 exists to prevent that shortcut. The existence of data is not the same as admissible authority to create consequence.

Why it matters

Execution failures often begin as evidence failures.

A system can execute the intended instruction and still misexecute if the evidence behind that instruction was incomplete, stale, broken, out of scope, unauthoritative, contaminated, or insufficient for reliance. That is why Evidence Governance sits upstream of Admissible Execution.

01

Bad evidence creates bad reliance

If the evidence is weak, unreliable, stale, or incomplete, reliance built on that evidence is unstable.

02

Bad reliance creates bad execution

When a system relies on evidence that has not become admissible, it may commit or execute without a legitimate basis.

03

Bad execution creates bad outcome

Once consequence attaches, the problem is no longer only informational. It becomes operational, institutional, environmental, financial, legal, or human.

The TA-14 evidence rule

No admissible evidence. No admissible reliance. No admissible execution.

Evidence Governance Architecture

Evidence itself requires governance.

Evidence Governance Architecture answers the upstream question: what must be true of evidence before admissibility can exist?

Source integrity Where did the record, signal, observation, measurement, log, output, claim, or determination originate, and is that origin trustworthy enough for review?
Chronology Was the sequence preserved from reality to record to evidence to determination, or did the system lose the order needed to support consequence?
Continuity Can the evidence prove it remained connected to the reality, system state, source event, or condition it claims to represent?
Sufficiency Is the evidence strong enough for the actual decision, route, intervention, reliance, or execution being considered?
Authority Who or what is authorized to generate, validate, interpret, rely on, bind, commit, or execute from this evidence?
Scope What is the evidence allowed to prove, and what is outside its evidentiary reach?
Survivability Can the evidence remain valid under runtime pressure, ambiguity, missing data, downstream reliance, adversarial pressure, or procedural momentum?
Lifecycle Is the evidence active, stale, superseded, degraded, conflicted, retired, or no longer reviewable?

Evidence Governance is upstream of execution

TA-14 remains Admissible Execution Architecture. Evidence Governance does not rename the parent. It explains what must be true before admissible execution can exist.

Evidence-to-admissibility transition

The key transition is not data to action. It is evidence to admissibility.

Many systems try to move from data directly to action. TA-14 inserts the missing governance sequence: record, evidence, admissible evidence, reliance, authority, binding, commit, execution, and outcome.

1

Record

A preserved trace, signal, document, observation, log, measurement, statement, image, output, or event record.

2

Evidence

A record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness to a claim or decision.

3

Admissible evidence

Evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, and authoritative enough to support a defined reliance condition.

4

Governed reliance

The controlled point where admissible evidence may support binding, commit, execution, or outcome claims within defined limits.

Evidence can outrun governance.

When evidence volume, speed, complexity, reliance pressure, or downstream use exceeds the system’s ability to preserve continuity and determine admissibility, the evidence chain can outrun governance. TA-14 treats that as a serious pre-execution risk.

Evidence states

Evidence has a state. That state can change.

TA-14 treats evidence as something that can be active, incomplete, degraded, conflicted, superseded, stale, or not reviewable. Execution should not rely on evidence without knowing its current state.

Active

Current enough, connected enough, and reviewable enough to participate in admissibility analysis.

Partial

Useful but incomplete. May support investigation, not consequence-bearing execution by itself.

Degraded

Weakening due to age, missing continuity, uncertainty, pressure, source issues, or ambiguity.

Conflicted

Competing records or interpretations prevent direct reliance without reconciliation or escalation.

Superseded

Replaced by stronger, newer, corrected, or higher-authority evidence.

Stale

Once useful but no longer current enough to support reliance for the proposed route.

Insufficient

Not strong enough to support the claim, threshold, diagnosis, authority, or execution route.

Not reviewable

Lacks enough origin, continuity, source, context, or preservation to participate in admissibility review.

Execution should not rely on unknown-state evidence

If the system does not know whether evidence is active, partial, degraded, conflicted, stale, superseded, insufficient, or not reviewable, then the route is not ready for consequence-bearing execution.

TA-14 chain mapping

Evidence Integrity supports every part of the admissible-execution chain.

Reality The condition, event, state, action, or environment the evidence claims to represent.
Record The preserved trace or source object that captures some aspect of reality.
Continuity The proof that the record remained connected, ordered, preserved, and reviewable.
Admissibility The determination that evidence is sufficient, scoped, authoritative, and reliable enough for reliance.
Binding The point where reliance begins to attach to obligation, permission, decision, routing, or consequence.
Commit The controlled boundary where a route becomes difficult to reverse and must be receipted.
Execution The action that may proceed only if the evidence chain supports admissible execution.
Outcome The preserved proof of what execution created, changed, prevented, escalated, or refused.

Boundaries

Evidence Integrity is not a claim that every record is ready for execution.

Not all records are evidence

A record can exist and still fail as evidence if source, context, continuity, or meaning cannot be established.

Not all evidence is admissible

Evidence can be relevant but still insufficient, stale, conflicted, unauthorized, degraded, or out of scope.

Not all admissible evidence authorizes execution

Even strong evidence must pass through reliance, authority, binding, commit, and execution control.

Not a legal evidence opinion

TA-14 evidence review is architecture and admissibility-chain review, not legal advice or a legal evidentiary ruling.

Not a cybersecurity audit

Evidence Governance can surface integrity issues, but it does not replace cybersecurity, forensic, or compliance audits.

Not free implementation scope

Custom schemas, route objects, implementation guidance, maturity roadmaps, and production mapping require written scope.

Correct positioning

Evidence Integrity is the upstream proof discipline inside TA-14. It governs whether the evidence chain is strong enough before a system is allowed to rely, bind, commit, execute, or create outcome.
