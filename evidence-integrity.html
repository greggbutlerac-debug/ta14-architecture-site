TA-14 Evidence Governance

Evidence must be governed before execution.

TA-14 does not begin at the moment of action. It begins earlier, with the evidence a system wants to rely on before it binds, commits, executes, or creates consequence.

Evidence Governance asks whether records, signals, logs, observations, measurements, outputs, claims, and determinations are strong enough to become admissible evidence — and whether admissible evidence is strong enough to support governed reliance.

Core Doctrine
A record is not automatically admissible evidence.

TA-14 separates record, evidence, admissible evidence, reliance, authority, binding, commit, execution, and outcome.

Evidence Must qualify
Admissibility Must be earned
Reliance Must be governed

The Distinction

Evidence does not become admissible just because it exists.

Many systems treat records, logs, signals, dashboards, measurements, outputs, or AI-generated content as if existence alone makes them reliable enough for action. TA-14 rejects that shortcut.

What many systems assume

  • If it is logged, it is evidence.
  • If it is evidence, it can be trusted.
  • If it can be trusted, it can be relied upon.
  • If it can be relied upon, execution may proceed.
  • If execution is logged, governance happened.

What TA-14 requires

  • A record must qualify as evidence.
  • Evidence must prove continuity and sufficiency.
  • Admissible evidence must be scoped to the decision.
  • Reliance must be authorized and bounded.
  • Execution must be controlled before consequence attaches.
The dangerous shortcut:

When systems skip from “a record exists” to “execution is allowed,” they collapse the evidence chain. The existence of data is not the same as admissible authority to create consequence.

Why It Matters

Execution failures often begin as evidence failures.

A system can execute the intended instruction and still misexecute if the evidence behind that instruction was incomplete, stale, broken, out of scope, unauthoritative, contaminated, or insufficient for reliance.

01

Bad evidence creates bad reliance.

If the evidence is weak, stale, incomplete, conflicted, or detached from reality, reliance built on it becomes unstable.

02

Bad reliance creates bad execution.

When a system relies on evidence that has not become admissible, it may bind, commit, or execute without a legitimate basis.

03

Bad execution creates bad outcome.

Once consequence attaches, the problem is no longer only informational. It becomes operational, institutional, environmental, financial, legal, or human.

No admissible evidence. No admissible reliance. No admissible execution.
Evidence Governance Architecture

Evidence itself requires governance.

Evidence Governance Architecture answers the upstream question: what must be true of evidence before admissibility can exist?

Source Integrity

Where did the record, signal, observation, output, claim, or determination originate?

Chronology

Was the sequence preserved from reality to record to evidence to determination?

Continuity

Can the evidence prove it remained connected, ordered, preserved, and reviewable?

Sufficiency

Is the evidence strong enough for the decision, route, intervention, reliance, or execution?

Authority

Who or what is authorized to generate, validate, interpret, rely, bind, commit, or execute?

Scope

What is the evidence allowed to prove, and what is outside its evidentiary reach?

Survivability

Can the evidence remain valid under runtime pressure, ambiguity, missing data, conflict, or downstream reliance?

Lifecycle

Is the evidence active, partial, degraded, conflicted, superseded, stale, insufficient, retired, or not reviewable?

Evidence Governance is upstream of execution.
TA-14 remains Admissible Execution Architecture. Evidence Governance does not rename the parent architecture. It explains what must be true before admissible execution can exist.
Evidence-to-Admissibility Transition

The key transition is not data to action. It is evidence to admissibility.

Many systems try to move from data directly to action. TA-14 inserts the missing governance sequence: Record → Evidence → Admissible Evidence → Governed Reliance → Authority → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome.

1

Record

A preserved trace, signal, document, observation, log, measurement, output, or event record.

2

Evidence

A record that can be evaluated for relevance, source, continuity, scope, meaning, and usefulness.

3

Admissible Evidence

Evidence strong enough, continuous enough, scoped enough, and authoritative enough to support reliance.

4

Governed Reliance

The controlled point where admissible evidence may support binding, commit, execution, refusal, escalation, or outcome claims.

Evidence can outrun governance when volume, speed, complexity, downstream use, automation pressure, dashboard reliance, AI-generated output, or institutional urgency exceeds the system’s ability to preserve continuity and determine admissibility.
Evidence States

Evidence has a state. That state can change.

TA-14 treats evidence as something that can be active, partial, degraded, conflicted, superseded, stale, insufficient, or not reviewable. Execution should not rely on evidence without knowing its current state.

Active

Current enough, connected enough, and reviewable enough to participate in admissibility analysis.

Partial

Useful but incomplete. May support investigation, but does not support consequence-bearing execution by itself.

Degraded

Weakening due to age, missing continuity, uncertainty, source issues, ambiguity, or pressure.

Conflicted

Competing records or interpretations prevent direct reliance without reconciliation.

Superseded

Replaced by stronger, newer, corrected, or higher-authority evidence.

Stale

Once useful but no longer current enough to support reliance for the proposed route.

Insufficient

Not strong enough to support the claim, threshold, diagnosis, authority, or execution route.

Not Reviewable

Lacks enough origin, continuity, source, context, or preservation to participate in review.

Execution should not rely on unknown-state evidence.

If the system does not know whether evidence is active, partial, degraded, conflicted, stale, superseded, insufficient, or not reviewable, the route is not ready for consequence-bearing execution.

TA-14 Chain Mapping

Evidence Governance supports every part of the admissible-execution chain.

Reality

The condition, event, state, action, or environment the evidence claims to represent.

Record

The preserved trace or source object that captures some aspect of reality.

Continuity

The proof that the record remained connected, ordered, preserved, and reviewable.

Admissibility

The determination that evidence is sufficient, scoped, authoritative, and reliable enough for reliance.

Binding

The point where reliance begins to attach to obligation, permission, decision, routing, or consequence.

Commit

The controlled boundary where a route becomes difficult to reverse and must be receipted.

Execution

The action that may proceed only if the evidence chain supports admissible execution.

Outcome

The preserved proof of what execution created, changed, prevented, escalated, or refused.
