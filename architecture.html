Architecture

The full-chain architecture for deciding whether execution should be allowed.

TA-14 Admissible Execution Architecture is a proof-bound governance architecture for consequence-bearing systems. It asks whether the entire route from real-world condition to outcome is evidenced, continuous, authorized, bounded, admissible, and accountable before action creates consequence.

Definition

TA-14 governs whether a system should be allowed to bind, commit, execute, or create reliance based on the evidence available right now.

Reality → Record → Continuity → Admissibility → Binding → Commit → Execution → Outcome

TA-14 is not just a gate.

A gate can stop or allow an action at one boundary. TA-14 governs the whole route that produced the proposed action: the reality being represented, the evidence record, the preservation of continuity, the admissibility threshold, the binding point, the commit boundary, the execution event, and the outcome record.

The problem

Modern systems can create consequence faster than evidence can govern it.

AI systems, institutional workflows, environmental evidence systems, partner architectures, automated recommendations, and operational decision routes can produce outputs, trigger actions, and create reliance before anyone proves that the underlying evidence is still valid. Monitoring, logging, policy language, dashboards, or after-the-fact reports may help, but they do not prove that consequence-bearing execution was admissible before it happened.

Monitoring is not governance

Monitoring may show conditions, events, or status. TA-14 asks whether the records behind action are preserved, continuous, thresholded, admissible, authorized, and accountable.

Logging is not admissibility

Audit logs may describe what happened. TA-14 asks whether the evidence chain should have allowed the action before execution created consequence.

Technical success is not execution legitimacy

A system can perform exactly as designed and still misexecute if it acted on stale evidence, invalid authority, weak continuity, or an ungoverned commit boundary.

Core TA-14 risk condition

A system may remain monitored, logged, policy-aligned, explainable, and technically functional while the admissible basis for consequence has already degraded. TA-14 exists to detect and govern that gap before action attaches consequence.

The governing stream

The TA-14 chain

TA-14 treats execution as the visible endpoint of a deeper evidentiary chain. Before a system acts, something was treated as real, recorded, preserved, relied upon, bound, committed, and executed. Each stage must remain admissible enough for the next stage to proceed.

1
Reality The real-world condition, event, environment, obligation, or operational fact the system claims to represent.
2
Record The captured evidence, source document, log, sensor reading, model output, diagnostic, or determination.
3
Continuity The preserved connection between reality and record across time, transfer, transformation, review, and use.
4
Admissibility The threshold finding that evidence is sufficient, current, scoped, authoritative, and reliable enough for reliance.
5
Binding The moment where information becomes reliance, reliance becomes obligation, or a determination constrains action.
6
Commit The controlled boundary where a person, system, workflow, agent, or institution commits to a consequence-bearing route.
7
Execution The action, intervention, release, refusal, escalation, routing, transaction, or decision that produces consequence.
8
Outcome The resulting state, consequence, verification record, accountability object, or new reality created by execution.

Architecture layers

TA-14 is an architecture family, not a single control.

TA-14 includes the parent admissible-execution chain and multiple supporting architecture families. These layers explain why TA-14 is broader than an audit trail, dashboard, compliance checklist, runtime gate, or safety policy.

Parent Admissible Execution Chain
Governs the full stream from Reality through Outcome. This is the core spine of TA-14.
Evidence Governance
Governs whether information can become evidence, whether evidence remains healthy and continuous, and whether it can support admissibility before reliance.
Reliance Governance
Governs what must be true before evidence, outputs, records, or determinations can be relied upon. Core principle: no admissible reliance, no admissible execution.
Authority Governance
Separates technical ability from admissible authority. A system may be able to act without having authority to create consequence.
Binding and Commit Governance
Identifies where evidence becomes reliance, where reliance becomes binding, and where binding moves into consequence-bearing commit.
Execution Governance
Controls whether action proceeds, narrows, pauses, escalates, refuses, or fails closed before consequence attaches.
Outcome Accountability
Preserves the result of execution, verifies post-action state, and records the new reality produced by action.
Institutional Memory
Preserves doctrine, review findings, lineage, changes, supersession, and publication boundaries so the TA-14 corpus remains governable over time.

Misexecution

A system can work and still misexecute.

TA-14 distinguishes ordinary technical failure from inadmissible consequence. A system can produce the expected output, follow its rules, pass a local check, and leave a log while still creating a consequence that should not have been allowed.

Technical failure

The system malfunctioned, crashed, violated a rule, produced an incorrect output, or failed to perform as designed.

Misexecution

The system acted or created reliance when the evidence, continuity, authority, scope, binding basis, commit decision, execution control, or outcome accountability was not admissible.

The TA-14 question is not only “did the system work?”

The deeper question is: should the system have been allowed to act, bind, commit, or create consequence on the evidence available at that moment?

Evidence Governance

Information is not evidence until it is governed.

A log, sensor reading, model output, report, diagnostic, checklist, document, or human statement does not automatically become admissible evidence merely because it exists. TA-14 treats evidence as governed infrastructure. It must be classified, preserved, connected, scoped, evaluated, and protected before it can support reliance.

Evidence continuity

Can the evidence still be traced back to the real condition, event, or source it claims to represent?

Evidence health

Is the evidence complete, current, coherent, uncorrupted, and suitable for the consequence involved?

Evidence capacity

Is the evidence strong enough to support the level of reliance, binding, commit, or execution being proposed?

Evidence survivability

Does the evidence remain admissible under runtime pressure, latency, drift, uncertainty, or changing conditions?

Evidence-to-admissibility transition

At what point does evidence become strong enough to support admissible reliance?

Evidence outrun condition

Has the system moved faster than its evidence can safely govern?

Reliance Governance

No admissible reliance. No admissible execution.

Evidence Governance asks what must be true before information can become admissible evidence. Reliance Governance asks what must be true before that evidence can be relied upon by a person, system, institution, workflow, partner architecture, or downstream process.

Admissible reliance requires more than available data.

  • The evidence must be current enough for the decision.
  • The authority must be valid for the scope of action.
  • The binding point must be visible and reviewable.
  • The commit boundary must be controlled.
  • The outcome must be accountable.

Reliance failure can happen before technical failure.

A system can operate exactly as designed while a user, institution, or downstream process relies on evidence that is not strong enough to support the consequence being created.

Boundaries

What TA-14 is not

TA-14 needs clear public boundaries because it can be misread if forced into familiar categories. These distinctions protect the architecture from being reduced to ordinary monitoring, audit logging, policy dashboards, or one-time execution gates.

TA-14 is not ordinary monitoring

Monitoring observes activity. TA-14 governs whether the evidentiary basis for action is admissible before consequence-bearing execution occurs.

TA-14 is not audit logging

Logs may show what happened. TA-14 asks whether what happened should have been allowed at all.

TA-14 is not a policy dashboard

Dashboards may display controls, obligations, or risk signals. TA-14 governs the chain through which evidence becomes reliance, commit, execution, and outcome.

TA-14 is not only an execution gate

TA-14 includes execution-boundary governance, but its scope begins at reality and continues through outcome.

Review use

How TA-14 is used in review

TA-14 reviews map systems, workflows, public materials, evidence packages, partner architectures, environmental processes, and institutional decision routes against the admissible-execution chain. The review posture is evidence-first, chronology-first, continuity-first, and strengths-plus-gaps.

Public-material architecture review

Maps public documentation, sites, repositories, claims, demos, and visible design evidence against the TA-14 chain without making unsupported claims about private capability.

Evidence gap review

Identifies missing records, continuity breaks, unclear authority, weak reliance basis, incomplete commit proof, or absent outcome verification before action or institutional reliance.

Production-readiness review

Evaluates whether consequence-bearing routes are governed from live reality through record, continuity, admissibility, binding, commit, execution, and outcome.
